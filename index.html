What This Actually Is

This isn't a hot take. It's a record.

Over six parts, this series has tracked one pattern: a Canadian politician says the right thing in public, then does the opposite when trade or votes are on the line. It started with the Komagata Maru in 1914. It runs straight through to a murder on Canadian soil in 2023, six expelled diplomats in 2024, and DOJ indictments unsealed in 2026.

The through-line is the Sikh Canadian community, because that's where the record is clearest and most documented. But the pattern isn't about one community. It's about what happens to any group a party treats as a vote bank instead of a constituency.

Foreign Interference

A foreign government secretly trying to shape Canadian politics, elections, or communities instead of dealing with Canada openly, through normal diplomacy. Open lobbying isn't interference. Secrecy and coercion are what make it interference.

Transnational Repression

A foreign government trying to silence, intimidate, or harm its own diaspora, people who left that country and now live somewhere else, including Canada. Documented forms include surveillance, threats to family back home, extortion, and in the most serious cases on record, violence.

The Pattern, Step by Step

This is the cycle this series keeps documenting, in every part, with different names attached to it.

The cycle doesn't break on its own. It's self-sealing. Every crisis becomes another photo opportunity. The only thing that's ever interrupted it is organized, public, on-the-record questions, asked by people who refuse to wait for permission.

Frequently Asked Questions

Straight answers, no jargon. If a term shows up in the series that isn't covered here, assume it's fair to ask.

Is this series anti-India, or anti-Hindu?

No. This series is aimed at specific, documented actions by state actors and at the Canadian politicians who stay silent about them. It is not aimed at a country's people or at a religion.

CSIS itself draws this same distinction: advocating for Khalistan is lawful political activity in Canada. The pattern this series documents is a strategy of collapsing that lawful advocacy into "extremism" to stigmatize an entire community. Naming that strategy is not an attack on Hindus or on India's people. It's a description of a specific playbook.

Why focus on Sikh Canadians specifically?

Because that's where the record is most documented right now: RCMP press conferences, a murder on Canadian soil, six expelled diplomats, unsealed DOJ indictments. It's checkable, not speculative.

But the argument of this series has always been that the pattern generalizes. Any community that a party can count on for votes without earning them, and can therefore stay quiet about when it's convenient, is vulnerable to the same cycle.

Is this series advocating for Khalistan?

No. This series documents political accountability. It is not a platform for any particular movement.

It does note, as a matter of Canadian law and CSIS's own public position, that advocating for an independent Sikh state is lawful political speech in Canada. Separately, it documents cases where foreign state actors have leaned on the existence of that advocacy to justify surveillance or repression of Sikh Canadians generally. Those are two different questions, and this series keeps them separate on purpose.

What's the source standard behind this series?

Every claim in this series traces to something a reader can check: RCMP statements, DOJ indictments, Elections Canada filings, court records, or established reporting from outlets including Baaz, the Globe and Mail, and CBC.

Unverifiable claims get removed, not softened. Disputed identifications are held out of publication entirely until they clear registry-level verification. This standard has cost the series speed more than once. It's kept every time.

Who runs this?

Project Aananta, an independent Substack. The person behind it has family who have served in the Canadian military, veterans and active service members both, which is part of why this series treats Canada's stated values as a standard worth holding it to, not a slogan to wave past.

Research throughout the series draws on original reporting from Baaz (@BaazNewsOrg), credited directly wherever their work is used.

What can I actually do about any of this?

Ask. Directly, publicly, and on the record.

Every Sikh MP currently sitting in Parliament is listed in this project's contact tool, with a ready-to-send template built around four checkable questions. You don't need to live in their riding. A Canadian's right to ask their representatives where they stand doesn't stop at a postal code.

Is this journalism, opinion, or something else?

Call it investigative commentary built on primary sources. Every factual claim is sourced. The framing, the pattern this series argues connects those facts together, is the author's own analysis, stated plainly as analysis rather than disguised as neutral reporting.
